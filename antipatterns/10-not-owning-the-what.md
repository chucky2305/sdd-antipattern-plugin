# Not owning the "what" in your spec

## Example

You describe your idea in a few sentences and let an AI produce the whole spec, then skim it and move on. Or you find a spec template online, swap in your product name, and call it done. Either way, the whats in the document (the problem, the users, the constraints, the tradeoffs) came from somewhere other than you.

## Why it's a problem

A spec that looks credible can still steer you in the wrong direction. An AI fills the gaps with generic assumptions that have nothing to do with your users, constraints, or market. A copied template carries the assumptions of whoever wrote it, for their product, their team, their context. If you didn't decide the whats yourself, you inherited someone else's, and you probably can't defend them.

There's a deeper problem too: deciding the whats is where you discover what you don't know yet. That's the part you can't hand off. Outsourcing it means outsourcing your understanding of your own product. This is the whole thesis of Sean Grove's "The New Code" talk: when the AI writes the code, the spec becomes the real work, and "the person who communicates most effectively is the most valuable programmer." Rubber-stamping an AI's whats without wrestling with them is the same failure in a nicer disguise.

## How to fix it

Own the what. You decide the problem, the users, the boundaries, and the tradeoffs, and you own them well enough to explain why each one is there. How you get to the words is up to you. It's fine to have an AI expand a tight brief into a fuller draft, or to borrow structure from another spec, as long as you interrogate every what it produces and rewrite anything you couldn't have written yourself. A good test: if someone pushed back on a line, could you defend it without saying "the AI suggested it"? If not, you don't own that part yet.
